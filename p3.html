journey begins

Coffee is a popular drink that comes from the seeds of the coffee plant. India is one of the top ten coffee producers in the world, and most of its coffee is grown in the southern states of Karnataka, Kerala, and Tamil Nadu. Coffee production in India involves several steps, from planting to roasting. The first step is to plant the coffee seeds in nurseries and then transplant them to the fields after about a year. The coffee plants are grown under the shade of trees, such as silver oak, jackfruit, and mango, to protect them from the sun and provide organic matter. The coffee plants also grow well with spices, such as pepper, cardamom, and vanilla, which add flavor and aroma to the beans.

birth of coffeee

The second step is to harvest the coffee fruits, which are called cherries. The cherries are picked by hand or by machines, depending on the terrain and accessibility of the fields. The cherries are then processed by either wet or dry methods to separate the beans from the pulp. The wet method involves washing the cherries in water and removing the pulp by mechanical or enzymatic means. The beans are then fermented in tanks or pits for a few hours to remove the mucilage, which is a sticky substance that covers the beans. The beans are then washed again and dried in the sun or by artificial means until they reach a moisture content of about 10%. The dry method involves spreading the cherries on large surfaces and drying them in the sun for several weeks. The cherries are turned regularly to ensure even drying and prevent mold growth.

final touch

The third step is to hull the dried beans to remove the outer layers, such as parchment, silver skin, and husk. The beans are then sorted by size, shape, color, density, and defects using machines or by hand. The beans are then graded according to their quality and characteristics. The highest grade of Indian coffee is called Plantation A, which consists of large and uniform Arabica beans. The fourth step is to roast the graded beans at different temperatures and durations to develop their flavor and aroma. The roasting process also reduces the moisture content and increases the oil content of the beans. The roasting level can range from light to dark, depending on the preference of the consumer. The ground coffee is then packed in airtight containers or bags to preserve its freshness and quality.

in stores

Coffee production in India is a labor-intensive and skillful activity that requires careful attention at every stage. Coffee growers in India face many challenges, such as climate change, pests and diseases, low prices, and competition from other countries. However, they also have many opportunities, such as increasing domestic consumption, diversifying into specialty coffees, adopting sustainable practices, and exploring new markets. Coffee production in India is not only a source of income for millions of people but also a way of life that reflects their culture and tradition.
